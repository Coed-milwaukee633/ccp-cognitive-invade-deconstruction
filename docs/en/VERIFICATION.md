## Detailed Verification

### [1] King, G., Pan, J., & Roberts, M. E. (2017)

**Cited claim**: The Chinese government produces approximately 448 million social media posts per year.

**Verification result**: ✅ Fully correct

- **Authors**: Gary King (Harvard University), Jennifer Pan (Stanford University), Margaret E. Roberts (UC San Diego). Correct.
- **Year**: Published 2017. Correct.
- **Journal**: *American Political Science Review*, Vol. 111, No. 3. Correct.
- **Pages**: 484–501. Correct.
- **Core data**: The paper's abstract explicitly states "the government fabricates and posts about 448 million social media comments a year." Correct.
- **DOI**: `10.1017/S0003055417000144`

**Primary source**: [Cambridge Core](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/american-political-science-review/article/how-the-chinese-government-fabricates-social-media-posts-for-strategic-distraction-not-engaged-argument/4662DB26E2685BAF1485F14369BD137C)

### [2] Google Threat Analysis Group / DRAGONBRIDGE (2024)

**Cited claim**: The DRAGONBRIDGE network had over 10,000 instances disrupted in Q1 2024 alone, with 175,000+ lifetime disruptions; active across 50+ platforms.

**Verification result**: ✅ Corrected (original version conflated two sources)

- **10,000+ instances (Q1 2024)**: Correct. From Google TAG blog post dated June 26, 2024, authored by Zak Butler.
- **175,000+ lifetime instances**: Correct. Same TAG article.
- **50+ platforms**: ⚠️ This figure came from **Meta's** August 2023 Adversarial Threat Report (cited by Ben Nimmo), not Google TAG. Google TAG only described DRAGONBRIDGE as active on "multiple platforms" without quantifying.
- **Spamouflage Dragon alias**: Correct. Google TAG article explicitly uses this alias. Original naming by Graphika (2019).

**Correction applied**: Re-attributed "50+ platforms" to Meta [4]; Google TAG section now only includes data from its own report.

**Primary source**: [Google TAG Blog](https://blog.google/threat-analysis-group/google-disrupted-dragonbridge-activity-q1-2024/)

### [4] Meta / Spamouflage CIB Reports (2019–2023)

**Cited claim**: Meta has been exposing the Spamouflage network since 2019, involving 7,704 accounts and 954 pages — the largest cross-platform covert influence operation Meta has ever disclosed.

**Verification result**: ✅ Largely correct

- **7,704 accounts / 954 pages**: Correct. From Meta's Q2 2023 Adversarial Threat Report. Also included 15 groups and 15 Instagram accounts (not mentioned in our text; not a critical omission).
- **"Largest" description**: Correct. Meta's official language was "the largest known cross-platform covert influence operation in the world" — actually stronger than our phrasing.
- **Since 2019**: Partially correct. The Spamouflage name was first used by Graphika in September 2019. Facebook conducted a small-scale takedown in August 2019 (5 accounts, 7 pages, 3 groups), but the major Spamouflage-branded takedown occurred in August 2023.

**Primary source**: [Meta Official Announcement](https://about.fb.com/news/2023/08/raising-online-defenses/); [Meta Transparency Report](https://transparency.meta.com/integrity-reports-q2-2023/)

### [5] Hille, K. / Financial Times (2019) — Want Want Group

**Cited claim**: Financial Times investigation revealed China Times accepted TAO instructions.

**Verification result**: ✅ Corrected (original conflated two separate sources)

- **Article existence**: Correct. Author Kathrin Hille, FT's Greater China correspondent.
- **Article title**: "Taiwan primaries highlight fears over China's political influence." Correct.
- **Date**: Published July 16, 2019 (London time); often cited as July 17 in Taiwan reporting. Corrected to July 16.
- **Content**: FT report was based on anonymous journalists alleging that TAO made "daily calls" influencing cross-strait coverage topics and angles. Want Want Group denied the allegations and filed (later dismissed) defamation suits. Text revised to "citing anonymous journalists alleging."
- **"Direct instructions" vs. "influence"**: Original text used "direct instructions," which was stronger than the original reporting's phrasing. Adjusted.

**Primary source**: [FT Article](https://www.ft.com/content/036b609a-a768-11e9-984c-fac8325aaa04); [RSF Report](https://rsf.org/en/taiwan-abusive-libel-suit-against-financial-times-correspondent)

### [6] Freedom House (2022) — Beijing's Global Media Influence

**Cited claim**: Freedom House Taiwan chapter documented Chinese capital penetrating Taiwan's media ecosystem through multiple channels.

**Verification result**: ✅ Fully correct

- **Report existence**: Correct. Full title: *Beijing's Global Media Influence 2022: Authoritarian Expansion and the Power of Democratic Resilience*, published September 8, 2022.

### [7][8][9] Taiwan Espionage Prosecution Statistics

**Cited claim**: Espionage prosecutions have surged dramatically in recent years, with approximately two-thirds involving military personnel.

**Verification result**: ✅ Corrected (original version conflated case counts with individual counts)

- **Original data source**: Taiwan's National Security Bureau report on "Analysis of Chinese Espionage Infiltration Tactics," released January 12, 2025.
- **Case counts**: 2021: 3 cases; 2022: 5 cases; 2023: 14 cases; 2024: 15 cases.
- **Individuals prosecuted**: 2021: 16; 2022: 10; 2023: 48; 2024: 64.
- **Original error**: The text stated "5, 14, 64 cases," conflating two metrics — 5 and 14 were case counts, but 64 was the number of individuals. Actual case count for 2024 was 15, not 64. The claim "more than triple the combined total of the previous two years" was also mathematically incorrect (cases: 5+14=19 > 15; individuals: 10+48=58, and 64 is only ~10% more). Corrected to show both metrics separately.
- **Two-thirds military**: Correct. Of the 64 individuals in 2024: 28 active-duty (43%) + 15 retired (23%) = 43 (approximately 67%).
- **Citation titles/years**: All three article titles in the original were fabricated; all three years were wrong (listed 2024, actually published 2025). Corrected to actual titles and dates.

**Primary sources**: [Taipei Times](https://www.taipeitimes.com/News/front/archives/2025/01/13/2003830100); [Jamestown Foundation](https://jamestown.org/program/taiwan-exposes-more-prc-military-infiltration-cases/); [CEIAS](https://ceias.eu/a-threat-from-within-chinese-espionage-in-taiwan/); [CNN](https://edition.cnn.com/2025/01/13/china/taiwan-surge-suspected-chinese-espionage-hnk-intl)

### [10] Lin Te-wang / RFA (2023)

**Cited claim**: In October 2023, Taiwan People's Communist Party founder Lin Te-wang was indicted for violating the Anti-Infiltration Act.

**Verification result**: ✅ Largely correct

- **Date**: Taipei District Prosecutors' Office filed charges on October 3, 2023. RFA report published October 4. Correct.
- **Person**: Lin Te-wang / Lin De-wang (different romanizations of the same Chinese name). Text describes him as "founder"; more precisely he is "founder and chairman." Acceptable.
- **Party name**: Taiwan People's Communist Party (TPCP). Correct.
- **Law**: Charges were filed under the Anti-Infiltration Act and the Civil Servants Election and Recall Act. Text only mentions the Anti-Infiltration Act — an acceptable simplification.
- **TAO connection**: Correct. Prosecutors alleged Lin had over a decade of contact with TAO Political Parties Bureau official Hu Chunguang.
- **Omissions**: Text does not mention co-defendant Vice Chairman Cheng Chien-hsin. Also note: on June 9, 2025, the Taipei District Court ruled not guilty.
- **RFA article**: Confirmed to exist, titled "Taiwan communist party leaders indicted for 'infiltration'."

**Primary source**: [RFA](https://www.rfa.org/english/news/china/taiwan-communist-infiltration-10042023041825.html); [Focus Taiwan](https://focustaiwan.tw/politics/202310030029)

### [14] Election Interference Statistics (originally attributed to MERICS)

**Cited claim**: The Ministry of Justice reported 77 suspected interference cases with 157 individuals investigated.

**Verification result**: ✅ Corrected (original had attribution error)

- **Data accuracy**: Correct. Deputy Minister of Justice Huang Mou-hsin announced the figures at a post-Executive Yuan meeting press conference on December 28, 2023.
- **Attribution error**: ❌ Original citation attributed this to MERICS. Upon verification, the MERICS article "In a savvy disinformation offensive, China takes aim at Taiwan election" **does not contain** these statistics. The actual source is a *Taipei Times* report from December 29, 2023, later cited by the Brookings article.
- **Data update**: 77 cases / 157 individuals was a snapshot from late December 2023. By January 11, 2024 (pre-election), the Supreme Prosecutors' Office reported updated figures of **117 cases / 287 individuals**. This has been added to the text.

**Primary source**: [Taipei Times](https://www.taipeitimes.com/News/front/archives/2023/12/29/2003811309); [Brookings](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/what-lessons-can-taiwan-share-with-the-world-on-election-interference/)

## Methodology Notes

1. **Verification scope**: Each citation was checked item-by-item for authors, year, title, publisher, and core data claims.
2. **Source hierarchy**: Primary sources (academic papers, official reports, platform announcements) were prioritized, with reliable news reporting used as secondary verification.
3. **Correction principle**: Errors were immediately corrected in the document itself, not merely flagged. The current document reflects all verification results.
4. **Limitations**: Some paywalled articles (e.g., FT) were cross-verified through secondary reporting. Citation titles that were paraphrases rather than exact titles have been corrected to original titles where possible.
